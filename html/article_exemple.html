Landkreuzer P.1500 “Monster”

In 1942 the Krupp company presented the most ambitious and impossible project ever designed in the history of weapons: the Lankreuzer P.1500 Monster.

History

Since the beginning of World War II, the purpose of the major automobile companies was to deliver heavy tank designs, which could convince the Führer. Among them were: Henschel, Krupp and Porsche. Each one competed by delivering increasingly better armed, armored and, above all, larger designs.

The tension between the companies ended at the end of 1942, when Krupp presented the P.1500 project, which he nicknamed "Monster". This machine weighing 1,500 tons, 12 meters high and 32 long, fully lived up to its name. Unlike other projects, the P.1500 was intended to be solely a means of transportation, as the company had previously designed the 800mm “Schwerer Gustav” K(E) railway gun.

Given the enormous difficulty involved in moving and installing this gigantic cannon by rail, Krupp believed that it was possible to design a platform that would help it move like a tank, so he created the P.1500 hull, which came in two different versions.

First Version

The first version was to leave the 80 cm gun as it was. But its rail platforms would be replaced by four tank hulls, all of which would be retrofitted so they could house a diesel engine. Specifically a MAN M9V 40/46, the same one found on Type IX submarines. It was a cheaper option, but much more difficult to operate.

Second Version

The second version consisted of installing the cannon on an armored hull, which would protect it from any threat. Armor up to 250mm thick would render anti-tank weapons useless, while a large array of 20mm MG-151/20 heavy machine guns would respond to a huge range of enemies. A pair of 15 cm sFH 18 howitzers would support it in combat against armored units. The cannon would be fixed in a single direction, so it had to completely rotate its chassis to be able to aim at other areas. This was a much more expensive option, but a bit easier to handle.

Due to its characteristics, P.1500 would be limited to fighting in open fields, since there would be no street or bridge that would allow it to cross to a specific point; and would require a constant supply of fuel, so it would only be used in extraordinary cases. It should be noted that the standard Schwerer Gustav required 1,500 men to operate properly, so the P.1,500 would require twice as many to remain operational.

End

Soon the project was inspected by the Minister of Armaments, Albert Speer, who in 1943 canceled the project, since it not only required an enormous amount of materials and capital, but also lacked tactical value. Soon the project was forgotten, and most of its information was lost. But the little that remains shows us that German megalomania seemed to have no end.
